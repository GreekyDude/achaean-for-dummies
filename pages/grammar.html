Grammar

Cases, declensions, verbs, and formulaic particles — the structures behind the tablets

Mycenaean Greek had 5 cases visible in the tablets — nominative, accusative, genitive, dative-locative, and instrumental. Because Linear B drops final consonants, cases are often hard to distinguish visually; context and ideograms do the work. All examples below are from the wanax paradigm, one of the best-attested noun paradigms in the corpus.

The verbal system in Linear B is poorly represented — the tablets are administrative records, not literature. Verbs appear mainly as infinitives, participles, and 3rd-person forms. The past-tense augment (the e- prefix marking past tense in classical Greek) is almost entirely absent. There is only one clearly augmented form in the entire corpus: a-pe-do-ke.

Certain short words and formulaic phrases recur across hundreds of tablets. Recognising them lets you parse an unfamiliar tablet quickly — even before you can read the content words.

SignsLinear BMeaning / Function
𐀵𐀰to-so"so much / so many" (tosos) — introduces a total. The most common tally word.
𐀵𐀰𐀆to-so-de"and so many" — running total with connective -de

𐀁𐀏e-ko"has/holds" (ekhei) — possession formula

Linear B was adapted from Linear A, a script designed for a non-Greek language. This mismatch forced scribes to develop orthographic workarounds. These rules are essential to reading — without them, words look unrecognisable. Sources: Wikipedia Linear B, Ventris & Chadwick Documents.
